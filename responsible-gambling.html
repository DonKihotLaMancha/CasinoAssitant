Understanding the Risks

Gambling involves financial risk and can become addictive. It's important to understand:

  • The house always wins: Casinos have a mathematical edge in all games
  • Losses are guaranteed: Over time, most gamblers lose money
  • Addiction is real: Problem gambling affects millions of people
  • It can destroy lives: Financial ruin, relationship problems, mental health issues

Signs of Problem Gambling

You may have a gambling problem if you:

  1. Spend more money or time gambling than you can afford
  2. Feel the need to gamble with increasing amounts of money
  3. Have tried unsuccessfully to stop or cut back
  4. Feel restless or irritable when trying to stop
  5. Gamble to escape problems or relieve feelings of helplessness
  6. Chase losses by gambling more to win back money
  7. Lie to family or friends about gambling
  8. Jeopardize relationships, job, or opportunities because of gambling
  9. Rely on others for money to relieve desperate financial situations
  10. Have committed illegal acts to finance gambling

üìã Self-Assessment Quiz

Answer these questions honestly:

1. Have you ever lost time from work or school due to gambling?
2. Has gambling made your home life unhappy?
3. Has gambling affected your reputation?
4. Have you ever felt remorse after gambling?
5. Have you ever gambled to get money to pay debts?
6. Has gambling decreased your ambition or efficiency?
7. After losing, do you feel you must return as soon as possible to win back losses?

If you answered "yes" to 3 or more questions, you may have a gambling problem and should seek help.

Tips for Responsible Gambling

‚úÖ DO:

  • Set a budget before you start and stick to it
  • Set a time limit for gambling sessions
  • Think of gambling as entertainment, not income
  • Only gamble with money you can afford to lose
  • Take regular breaks
  • Balance gambling with other activities
  • Keep track of time and money spent
  • Accept losses as the cost of entertainment

‚ùå DON'T:

  • Gamble when depressed, upset, or in pain
  • Chase losses by gambling more
  • Borrow money to gamble
  • Use gambling to solve financial problems
  • Gamble under the influence of alcohol or drugs
  • Skip work, school, or family time to gamble
  • Lie about gambling habits
  • Gamble with money needed for bills or essentials

Self-Exclusion Programs

Many casinos and online gambling sites offer self-exclusion programs that allow you to voluntarily ban yourself from gambling for a set period. Contact individual casinos or visit:

  • National Self-Exclusion Database: Available in many jurisdictions
  • GamStop (UK): www.gamstop.co.uk
  • State Programs: Check your state's gaming commission website

Treatment Options

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Helps change unhealthy gambling behaviors
  • Motivational Interviewing: Helps resolve ambivalence about treatment
  • Medication: Some medications may help reduce urges
  • Support Groups: Gamblers Anonymous and similar programs
  • Residential Treatment: Intensive programs for severe addiction
  • Financial Counseling: Help managing debt and rebuilding finances

For Family and Friends

If someone you know has a gambling problem:

  • Express concern without judgment
  • Encourage them to seek professional help
  • Don't lend money or pay their gambling debts
  • Attend Gam-Anon meetings for support
  • Take care of your own mental health
  • Set boundaries to protect yourself
